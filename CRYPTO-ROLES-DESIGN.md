# Cryptographic Role Enforcement — Design (resolving audit finding R-1)

**Status:** IMPLEMENTED (Option B, with the rUnderF refinement: existing caregiver/MFA/recovery wraps unchanged; DEK_R stored wrapped under DEK_F). Core proven in tests/zone-core-test.mjs (16 assertions); integrated into unlock, save routing, outbox ingestion, zone-aware blob packaging/ingestion, setup, passcode update, and all MFA key-rewrite paths. See README "Cryptographic Role Scoping" and CHANGES-SINCE-REVIEW Round 5.

## The honest answer to "can crypto fix R-1?"

Partially, and the part it can fix is the part that matters. Cryptography enforces *read access*: who can turn ciphertext into plaintext. It cannot enforce *behavioral* distinctions among people who legitimately decrypt the same data — admin vs. family vs. care professional all need to read essentially the same records to do their jobs, so their differences (who may delete an incident, who may export) will always be UI-level in a serverless app; any of them, suitably skilled, can do anything with data they can decrypt. No local-first design changes that.

What cryptography *can* enforce is the boundary the product actually cares most about: the **client-restricted tier**. Today this boundary is weaker than it looks — the client passcode wrap (`wk.r`) unwraps the *same DEK* as the caregiver wrap, so a client-restricted session holds the key to the entire vault and only rendering stands between the person with dementia (or anyone who learns the simpler client passcode) and the capacity logs, POA decisions, finances, and after-death planning. The fix makes that boundary mathematical: the client passcode unwraps a key that decrypts only the client-visible zone, and the private zone becomes as unreachable to it as to someone with no passcode at all. Note that `client-full` ("Independent") is deliberately out of scope: that tier exists for a person managing their own care, who is entitled to their own legal and financial data, so the household's tier choice at setup determines whether the client wrap contains the full bundle or the restricted key only.

This also matters for the institutional track: the same primitive — a scoped key that decrypts a defined projection — is exactly the mechanism the unresolved care-navigator consent-visibility question needs. A navigator could hold a key to a consented zone rather than a UI promise. Designing the two-tier version well creates the pattern.

## The design (proven core)

Two independent 256-bit keys: `DEK_F` (full/private zone) and `DEK_R` (restricted/client zone). The caregiver passcode — and the MFA combined-factor key, unchanged in scheme — wraps the bundle `{F,R}`; a client-restricted passcode wraps `{R}` alone. The vault envelope becomes two ciphertexts, `ctF` and `ctR`, produced by a deterministic top-level split: client-visible roots (selfReports, appointments, medSchedule, emergencyPlans, messages, plus a whitelisted subset of settings fields) go to R; everything else — incidents, expenses, capacityLog, poaDecisions, savedDocs, domains, audit anchor, MFA metadata — stays in F. The exact root list is fixed by an audit of what client views actually render (the permission map at `can()` makes this enumerable) and is the main piece of careful product work; the split/join itself is proven lossless.

The invariant that makes a client session safe is **opaque carry-forward**: a client session reads, modifies, and re-encrypts only `ctR`, and copies `ctF` forward byte-identical. It cannot corrupt what it cannot read; AES-GCM authentication means it also cannot undetectably tamper. Both properties are in the proof suite, as is the round-trip where a client's new self-report becomes visible to the next caregiver session alongside intact private data.

## Two integration options

**Option A — full zone partition.** Both zones live natively in the storage engine: snapshots hold `{ctF, ctR}`, every WAL entry routes its diff by zone root into separately encrypted sub-diffs, sync payloads carry zones separately, and blobs are encrypted under the zone key of their owning record. This is the architecturally complete version, and the expensive one: it reopens the WAL (the most heavily proven subsystem), requires zone-aware replay and checkpointing on both tiers, and introduces a sharp hazard this design pass already identified — blob garbage collection must be zone-scoped, because a client session's mark-and-sweep would otherwise see every F-zone blob as "unreferenced" (its state lacks the referencing records) and securely delete the family's incident photos. Solvable (zone-tagged blob ids; client GC touches only R-tagged blobs), but it is exactly the kind of cross-cutting invariant that makes Option A a re-prove-everything project. Estimated at 1.5–2× the write-ahead-log effort, touching nearly every proven path, mid-V2-build.

**Option B — encrypted client projection (recommended).** The caregiver-tier pipeline — single DEK, WAL, snapshots, sync, GC, MFA — stays exactly as it is today, untouched and still covered by its proofs. What changes: every caregiver-session save additionally maintains a small **client projection** — the R-zone subset, encrypted under `DEK_R` — alongside the vault, and the client passcode wraps `{R}` only. A client session decrypts just the projection, renders through the existing restricted views, and writes its self-reports (with media encrypted under `DEK_R`) into an append-only **outbox**, also under `DEK_R`. The next caregiver session on the device ingests the outbox into the main vault through the normal externalize/sanitize path. On a shared household device — the dominant deployment for this tier — freshness is identical to today, since a caregiver had to unlock to see client submissions anyway. The R-1 security property is the same as Option A's: the client passcode cannot decrypt private data, full stop. What Option B gives up is architectural purity (the private zone isn't natively partitioned; a caregiver session still operates on one joined state) and native zone-aware *sync* for client devices — acceptable, because the team sync passcode is a caregiver-tier credential and client-only devices don't hold it. Estimated at roughly 10–15% of Option A: a projection writer on the save path, a client unlock path, an outbox ingester, zone-scoped handling for the (few) client-zone blobs, migration, and an extension of the zone-core proof to the projection/outbox flow.

Migration in either option is automatic and never in place, per the standing policy: on first caregiver unlock of an old vault, generate `DEK_R`, write the new envelope/projection to the inactive slot, verify, flip, and re-wrap the client passcode to `{R}` (when the tier is restricted). Old backups continue to import through the existing paths.

## Limits to state plainly (in docs and to buyers)

Three things this fix does not do. It does not protect against a restricted user who learns a *caregiver* passcode — no key hierarchy can. It does not create cryptographic distinctions among caregiver-tier roles, for the reasons above; those remain UI-level and the documentation should keep saying so. And on a shared device, plaintext the caregiver decrypted in a still-open session is in memory regardless of key hierarchies — lock hygiene (now including the media-cache clear) remains the control there. With those stated, R-1's wording would change from "role enforcement is UI-level" to "the client-restricted boundary is cryptographically enforced; intra-caregiver role distinctions are UI-level by design."

## Recommendation

Implement Option B now: it delivers the entire security property of the question being asked, leaves every proven subsystem untouched, fits the V2 timeline, and establishes the scoped-key pattern the navigator-consent feature will need. Hold Option A as the documented evolution path if a future requirement (client-tier multi-device sync, navigator zones at scale) demands native partitioning — at which point the zone-core proofs and the GC-scoping hazard analysis here are the starting point rather than discoveries.
